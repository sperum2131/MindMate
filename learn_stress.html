Exploring Stress

Learn what stress is, how it affects your body and mind, and how to manage it effectively.

What is Stress?

Stress is a natural reaction routed deep in the human body. When you experience challenges or changes (stressors), your body produces physical and mental responses that we know as stress.

Stress causes your body to adjust to new situations. It can be positive by keeping you alert and ready to avoid danger. For example, if you were stressed about a test you would be motivated to study for it. Stress can also be negative if it continues without periods of relief or relaxation.

Types of Stress

There are three main kinds of stress:

  • Acute stress: Short-term stress that comes and goes quickly, either positive or negative. For instance, this is the stress you experience when you go on a roller-coaster.
  • Episodic acute stress: Acute stress that persists on a regular basis. You never return to a relaxed state or experience any relief. Typically, this occurs in certain professionals like healthcare providers.
  • Chronic stress: Long-term stress that goes on for weeks or months and never stops. This may be due to marriage troubles, issues at work or financial problems. This is widely regarded as the most harmful type of stress.

How Stress Affects Your Body

Your body's autonomic nervous system controls your heart rate, breathing, vision changes, and more. Its built-in response, the "flight or fight" response, helps deal with stressful situations.

Sustained chronic stress can keep this system running, leaving profound impacts on your body. These can be physical, psychological, or behavioral.

Physical symptoms may include:

  • Aches and pains
  • Chest pain or a feeling like your heart is racing
  • Exhaustion or trouble sleeping
  • Headaches, dizziness or shaking
  • High blood pressure (hypertension)
  • Muscle tension or jaw clenching
  • Stomach or digestive problems
  • Weakened immune system

Psychological symptoms can include:

  • Anxiety or irritability
  • Depression
  • Panic attacks
  • Sadness

Behavioral symptoms can include:

  • Alcohol use disorder
  • Gambling disorder
  • Overeating or developing an eating disorder
  • Participating compulsively in sex, shopping or internet browsing
  • Smoking
  • Substance use disorder

What Causes Stress?

Stress is your body's reaction to a change or challenge. Stressors can be both positive and negative. Some causes of stress include:

  • An illness or death of a loved one
  • Marriage, separation or divorce
  • Financial issues
  • Moving to a new house
  • Having a baby
  • Studying for a big test
  • Getting ready for a work presentation
  • Having an argument with a friend or loved one

Complications of Chronic Stress

Chronic stress can lead to many long-term health issues affecting your:

  • Immune system: Like arthritis, fibromyalgia and psoriasis
  • Digestive system: Like weight gain or loss, ulcers and irritable bowel syndrome
  • Cardiovascular system: Like high blood pressure, increased heart rate and heart palpitations
  • Reproductive system: Like infections, polycystic ovarian syndrome and infertility

Stress can also increase your likelihood of developing a mental health condition, such as depression or anxiety. Please call or text 988 to reach the 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline if you feel extremely stressed or think about hurting yourself.

How to Manage Stress

Stress is unavoidable, but it can be relieved through these methods:

  • Get physical activity: Taking a quick walk, working out, or stretching can help relieve stress.
  • Practice relaxation activities: Try meditation, yoga, breathing exercises and muscle relaxation.
  • Take good care of your body: Eat right, participate in physical activity, and get enough sleep.
  • Stay positive and practice gratitude: Reflect on the good parts of your day or life.
  • Accept that you can't control everything: Find ways to let go of worry about situations you can't change.
  • Learn to say "no": Do not take on additional responsibilities when you're too busy or stressed.
  • Stay connected: Maintain relationships with people who keep you calm, make you happy, provide emotional support and help you with practical things.
  • Consider talking to a therapist: A healthcare provider can help by offering advice, prescribing medicine or referring you to a mental health professional.

Stress is natural, and everyone has it. However, long-term stress can cause physical, emotional and behavioral symptoms. It is important to learn how to manage it effectively.
